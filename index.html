Why then

The city empties out. The food turns to cold-weather food. And the Amarone in the risotto is drying on wooden racks twenty minutes away while we eat it — that only happens between October and December.

Buildings

— Castelvecchio. Scarpa cut the castle open and left the cuts showing.

— Banca Popolare, Piazza Nogara. His last building. Ten minutes' walk, still a working bank, so we look from outside. Roman crushed-ceramic plaster on a 1970s façade.

— San Zeno Maggiore. Romanesque. The bronze doors are the thing.

— San Fermo Maggiore. Two churches stacked on top of each other.

— Porta Borsari and Ponte Pietra. Roman, standing in the street, no ticket.

— Palazzo Bevilacqua, Corso Cavour. Sanmicheli. On the way to everything else.

— Giardino Giusti. Renaissance garden, cypresses, a hedge maze. (if there are four of us, this first)

Three museums

— Museo di Castelvecchio. The Cangrande on its concrete plinth. The reason we're going.

— Palazzo Maffei. A stone spiral staircase that runs cellar to roof holding itself up. Rooftop over Piazza delle Erbe.

— Museo Archeologico al Teatro Romano. A Roman theatre cut into the hill, a monastery above it, and the best view of the city.

Where to eat

— Antica Bottega del Vino. Risotto all'Amarone and a wine list from another century. Book this one.

— Trattoria Al Bersagliere. Bollito con la pearà in a 13th-century cellar.

— Ostregheteria Sottoriva 23. Under the medieval porticoes on the best street in the city. Small plates, no booking.

— Trattoria al Pompiere. The cured meat counter, then the tiramisu.

— Osteria del Bugiardo. A glass of Valpolicella standing up, between things.

— Greppia. Family-run, home cooking, bigoli with duck ragù. (if there are four of us, this one)
